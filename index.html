About Me

Hi, I am Dawei Zhong. I won my Bachelor of Science degree at Department of Astronomy, Xiamen Universiy, and I am taking my gap year to develop my understanding of cosmology and dark matter.

My current professional interest lie in the cutting-edge of physics and astronomy: Dark Matter. Dark matter accounts for about 85% of the matter in the universe. Its presence has been proved by a couple of astronomical observations indirectly, but scientists have no idea about what it really is. I am curious about how the mysterious matter affects the evolution of the universe and how we can find the "smoking gun" of dark matter via advanced telescopes.

My undergraduate work mainly focus on hot, highly-ionized circumgalactic medium. Most previous hot CGM observations concentrate on the abundant hot gas (helium-like ion O VII) inside or around the Milky Way. My collaborator and I focus on less frequently studied hot gas called O VIII (with only one electron outside the oxygen nucleus).

My Research Project

Prospects for Dark Matter Astronomy

Advisor: Prof. Kevork Abazajian

This is our estimation of dark matter emission intensity in the Milky Way (left: annihilation emission; right: decay emission). We are prepared for the coming era of "Dark Matter Astronomy"!

Human beings have make sevearl progresses to the understanding of dark matter. Scientists have proposed various kinds of particle candidates. They also spare no effort to search the smoking gun for dark matter from both particle physics and astromocial sides. Currently, particle observers find nothing in ground-based dark matter experiments. In contrast, astronomers contribute a lot in the game. They discovered unresolved X-ray and gamma-ray emission, which are explained by some kinds of dark matter radiation. Although these findings do not constitute a discovery of dark matter, we also believe the possibility to find DM direct signals from the sky. With the help of future advanced detectors such as X-ray telescope XRISM, Athena, and gamma-ray telescope eASTROGAM, scientists might successfully identify dark matter distinctive features and study their particle properties.

In this project, I concentrate on dark matter in the Milky Way, investigating its observables and discussing the ability of future detectors to find the signals. We consider both DM annihilation and decay processes for photon production. We also examine the centroid shift (left) and broadening pattern (right) of DM narrow emission features (see above).

More than theoretical calculation, we also compare the radial velocity (move away or towards us) between dark matter (solid lines) and astrophysical sources (dash lines). It is worth mentioning that if using future Athena X-ray Observatory, we could detect a separation of two spectral resolution bins for an unresolved emission line at l = 90 and l = 270.

Please refer to my iPoster or here for more details about the project.

XMM-Newton Survey for Local O VIII Absorber

Advisor: Prof. Taotao Fang

This is the first and most comprehensive study for the O VIII absorption properties inside and around the Milky Way, and help us stand closer to the understanding of hot gaseous corona of galaxies and the solution of the missing Galactic baryon problem.

Together with my collaborator Zheng Zhou, we conducted an all-sky survey of hydrogen-like O VIII absorber in totally 99 directions (see the Figure above). The temperature of these highly-ionzied gas is about millions degree, and the most abundant component has been greatly studied. In contrast with uniformly distributed O VII, we found most O VIII locates near the Galactic plane. Our result support the disk-concentrated gas distribution model.

Local Hot Gas Along the H 2356-309 Sight-line

Advisor: Prof. Taotao Fang

We found in the survey project an evidence that indicates the Milky Way has a multiphase hot gas corona. Previous researches believe that the hot gas are iso-thermal (or the temperature is the same everywhere). The H 2356-309 sight-line has a 3.1 sigma O VIII and 1.9 sigma O VII, and the measured temperature is 2.3Ã—106 K. This is higher than the sight-line of Mkn 421, PKS 2155-304 and 3C 273. Also, base on our calculation result, it challenges the assumption that O VII and O VIII have same spatial distribution, and suggested that O VIII has a significant longer path length extending into Galactic halo.

Note that Das et al. (2019) also detect Ne X absorption line along the 1ES 1553+113 sight-line, and it indicates the X-ray absorber has two temperatures here (see Das et al. 2019). Since this sight-line is near the Loop I structure while our detection would not on any X-ray structures, our finding would be a much better indicator of pure multiphase CGM.
